Your Diabetes Care Guide

If you have been told your A1C is high or that you have diabetes, this guide will help you understand the next steps to protect your health.

  • Endocrinology for specialized diabetes care
  • A dietitian for nutrition support

Preventing Diabetes Complications

Keeping your blood sugar in a healthy range can help protect your body and prevent long-term complications.

  • Manage your blood sugar levels
  • Eat healthy foods
  • Get regular physical activity
  • Take medications as prescribed
  • Attend recommended medical appointments

These steps help protect your:

  • 👁 Eyes
  • 🦶 Feet and nerves
  • 🩸 Kidneys
  • ❤️ Heart and blood vessels

Diabetes Complications

Over time, high blood sugar can damage different parts of the body. Many complications develop slowly and may not cause symptoms at first, which is why regular checkups and screenings are important.

👁 Diabetic Retinopathy

Diabetes can damage the small blood vessels in the retina. This condition is called diabetic retinopathy and can lead to vision loss if it is not detected early.

→ Go to Eye Care

🦶 Diabetic Foot Wounds

Diabetes can damage nerves and reduce blood flow in the feet. This can lead to loss of feeling, sores, and infections that may take longer to heal.

→ Go to Foot Care

🩸 Kidney Disease

Diabetes can damage the kidneys over time. Kidney disease may not cause symptoms early, so regular testing is important.

→ Go to Kidney Care

👁 Eye Care

Diabetes can damage the small blood vessels in the retina, the light-sensitive tissue at the back of the eye. This condition is called diabetic retinopathy.

People with type 2 diabetes should have a dilated eye exam when they are diagnosed and then as recommended by their eye doctor.

Call your provider if you notice:

  • Sudden vision changes
  • Dark spots or floaters
  • Blurred vision that does not improve

🦶 Foot Care & Neuropathy

Diabetes can damage nerves and reduce blood flow in the feet. This is called diabetic neuropathy. When nerve damage occurs, people may lose feeling in their feet and may not notice injuries such as cuts, blisters, or sores.

Because wounds may heal more slowly in people with diabetes, small injuries can sometimes lead to infections or serious foot problems if they are not treated early.

Foot care tips:

  • Check your feet every day for cuts, blisters, redness, or swelling
  • Look between your toes and on the bottoms of your feet
  • Wear comfortable, well-fitting shoes
  • Keep feet clean and dry
  • Tell your provider if you notice sores, numbness, or tingling

Your provider may recommend a foot exam each year to check circulation and nerve sensation.

🩸 Kidney Care

Diabetes can damage the kidneys over time. This condition is called diabetic kidney disease.

The kidneys help filter waste from the blood. When they are damaged, protein can leak into the urine and the kidneys may not work as well. Kidney problems often do not cause symptoms early, which is why regular testing is important.

Your provider may monitor kidney health using:

  • Blood tests to measure kidney function
  • Urine tests to check for protein

People with type 2 diabetes usually begin kidney screening at the time of diagnosis and continue testing regularly.

Call your provider if you notice:

  • Swelling in the legs or feet
  • Changes in urination
  • Persistent fatigue
