Welcome to Your Diabetes Care Guide

If you have been told your A1C is high or that you have diabetes, this guide will help you understand the next steps to protect your health.

Be sure to make an appointment with:

  • Your primary care provider
  • An eye doctor for a dilated eye exam

You may also receive a referral to:

  • Podiatry for foot care
  • Endocrinology for specialized diabetes care
  • A dietitian for nutrition support

Preventing Diabetes Complications

Keeping your blood sugar in a healthy range can help protect your body and prevent long-term complications.

  • Manage your blood sugar levels
  • Eat healthy foods
  • Get regular physical activity
  • Take medications as prescribed
  • Attend recommended medical appointments

These steps help protect your:

  • 👁 Eyes
  • 🦶 Feet and nerves
  • 🩸 Kidneys
  • ❤️ Heart and blood vessels

Diabetes Complications

👁 Diabetic Retinopathy

Diabetes can damage small blood vessels in the retina and lead to vision loss if not detected early.

🦶 Diabetic Foot Wounds

Diabetes can damage nerves and reduce blood flow in the feet, which can lead to wounds that heal slowly.

🩸 Kidney Disease

Diabetes can damage the kidneys over time. Regular testing can help detect kidney problems early.

👁 Eye Care

Diabetes can damage blood vessels in the retina. This condition is called diabetic retinopathy.

People with type 2 diabetes should have a dilated eye exam when diagnosed and then as recommended by their eye doctor.

Call your provider if you notice:

  • Sudden vision changes
  • Dark spots or floaters
  • Blurred vision that does not improve

🦶 Foot Care & Neuropathy

Diabetes can damage nerves and reduce blood flow in the feet. This is called diabetic neuropathy.

Foot care tips:

  • Check your feet daily
  • Look for cuts, blisters, redness, or swelling
  • Wear comfortable shoes
  • Keep feet clean and dry
  • Tell your provider if you notice sores or numbness

🩸 Kidney Care

Diabetes can damage the kidneys over time. This is called diabetic kidney disease.

Your provider may monitor kidney health using:

  • Blood tests
  • Urine tests to check for protein

People with type 2 diabetes usually begin kidney screening at diagnosis and continue testing regularly.
